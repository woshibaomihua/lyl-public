CADR vs ACH for air purifier buyers: how to choose the right model

Target buyer: distributors and project buyersTopic: airflow selectionSEO landing article

CADR and ACH are two of the most important concepts in air purifier sourcing. Buyers who understand both can choose products more accurately and avoid underpowered models.

CADR stands for clean air delivery rate. It usually describes how much filtered air a purifier can deliver over a given time. ACH stands for air changes per hour. It describes how many times the air in a room is theoretically cleaned or replaced in one hour. A product can look strong in a brochure, but if the CADR is too low for the room size, the buyer’s customer may feel the product is weak. On the other hand, an oversized model may be too expensive, noisy or difficult to position in a retail lineup.

Why CADR matters for B2B buyers

Retailers and distributors use CADR to segment products. A small bedroom model, a large-room purifier, an office smoke odor unit and a school or clinic purifier should not be described in the same way. If a buyer plans to sell through e-commerce, CADR helps create product comparison tables. If the buyer serves projects, CADR helps estimate how many units are needed for a room, office, classroom or public area.

CADR also affects claims and expectations. Buyers should avoid vague claims such as “suitable for all rooms.” A better product page explains recommended room size, target ACH, filter type, noise level, replacement filter and usage scenario. This is why the website framework includes both product pages and a CADR/ACH educational guide.

Understanding ACH

ACH can be estimated with a simple formula: room area multiplied by ceiling height gives room volume. Multiply the room volume by the desired air changes per hour to estimate required airflow. For example, a 30 m² room with a 2.6 m ceiling has a volume of 78 m³. At 4 ACH, the target airflow is about 312 m³/h. This does not replace formal testing, but it gives buyers a practical selection starting point.

For bedrooms, buyers may accept lower ACH if quiet sleep mode is the primary need. For pet odor, smoke, office and commercial applications, buyers may need higher airflow and stronger carbon filtration. For schools, clinics or project buyers, final selection should consider safety, documents, installation layout, room usage and maintenance plan.

Filter package and real-world performance

CADR is not the only factor. HEPA grade, activated carbon weight, pre-filter design, sealing, fan design, noise control and filter replacement cycle all matter. A high airflow unit with a weak filter may not satisfy buyers who need pollen, dust, smoke or odor control. A very dense filter may reduce airflow if the fan system is not designed properly. This is why B2B buyers should request spec sheets and test data where available.

For UV-C or disinfection products, buyers should also confirm safety instructions, wavelength, exposure design, product usage restrictions and warning labels. Marketing claims should match documentation and target market rules. A responsible supplier will help buyers choose conservative, document-supported language.

How to use CADR in product selection

  1. Define target room type: bedroom, living room, office, school, clinic, HVAC duct or commercial space.
  2. Estimate room volume and desired ACH.
  3. Shortlist products by airflow and recommended coverage.
  4. Check noise level for the intended use.
  5. Confirm filter package and replacement cost.
  6. Request model-specific certificates and spec sheet.
  7. Test samples before bulk order.

How CADR affects product line planning

Importers usually need more than one CADR level. A small model can target bedrooms and low-price channels. A mid-size model can target living rooms, pet households and offices. A higher airflow model can target commercial or project demand. If all products in a catalog look similar, the buyer will struggle to build a clear price ladder. CADR segmentation helps create good-better-best product architecture.

Questions buyers should ask about CADR

Ask whether the airflow figure is tested, estimated or listed as fan airflow. Ask whether the filter is installed during the test. Ask about noise level at each fan speed, because a purifier may have impressive airflow only at a noisy maximum setting. Ask about replacement filter cost, because a high-CADR model with expensive filters may be harder to sell in some markets.

For project buyers, CADR should be discussed with the room layout and usage scenario. One large unit may not always perform better than several smaller units distributed across a room. Final decisions should also consider maintenance access, safety, power supply, noise and user behavior.
