How buyers should use this page

Certification names should not be treated as universal claims across every product variant. For each RFQ, the buyer and supplier should confirm the exact model number, electrical version, plug type, target market, packaging language and final SKU configuration. The sales team should then provide the correct certificate scan, test report or declaration for that configuration.

For the United States, buyers commonly ask about FCC, EPA/CARB related documents and retailer listing requirements. For the European Union, buyers usually request CE, RoHS, GS or related compliance files. For project or institutional procurement, buyers may also request factory audit documents, ISO9001 files, quality inspection photos and third-party inspection support.

Model-specific certificate mapping

The certificate download center should eventually map each document to a specific product model. For example, the 80W humidifier and UV air purifier may have one document set, while the commercial UV-C cart and HVAC bipolar ionizer may require a different set. This prevents buyers from assuming that one certificate applies to every item in the catalog.

For each PDF upload, the recommended file naming format is: model-name_certificate-type_market_year.pdf. A good certificate card should show the certificate type, applicable model, target market, issue date if available, and whether the document is a full report, declaration, registration document or reference scan. If the certificate is sensitive, the page can ask buyers to submit a company email before receiving the file.

Certificate request workflow

  1. Buyer selects the product model and target country.
  2. Sales team checks which documents match the exact SKU configuration.
  3. Buyer receives the certificate scan, report or declaration by email.
  4. For retail or institutional projects, buyer confirms whether further local testing is required.

This careful wording is especially important for UV-C, sterilization and air quality claims. The site should avoid exaggerated medical or sterilization claims unless they are supported by model-specific documents and approved market language.
